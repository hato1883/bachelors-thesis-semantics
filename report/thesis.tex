\chapter{Introduction}
\label{cha:introduction}
As software becomes more critical, \todo{Is this a good funnel into topic?}
and the amount of programming languages increases, the need to ensure
an instruction in a program does what the programmer think it does becomes essential.
This is especially important when compilers and interpreters translate code into
lower level representations such as machine code or bytecode.

To bridge this gap of syntax and semantics, the field of programming language \todo{is the term "gap" applicable here}
semantics was developed, formalizing the behavior of instructions
in a rigorous mathematical representation.
By defining these meanings, we can reason about a program's behavior and
validate that it works as intended regardless of input.

In academic settings, such as the course
"Semantics of Programming Languages (1DL311)" provided by Uppsala University, \todo{Course or the litterature itself}
these definitions and proofs are often presented "on paper" using
techniques such as induction over the structure.
These paper-based proofs frequently omit inductive steps that share
similarities with previous ones. While these proofs are useful for
teaching, it is also susceptible to human error, implicit assumptions and
gaps in logical reasoning. By mechanizing the proof we introduce a layer of
automatic validation, thereby ensuring logical soundness and verify
formal correctness.

This thesis investigates the process of translating these pen-and-paper proofs
into mechanized proofs using the proof assistant Lean. The goal is to
evaluate whether the techniques used in the 1DL311 curriculum correspond
directly to Lean's mechanisms or if they require any reformulation to achieve
machine-level precision.

\label{intro:research_question}
\section{Research Question and Goals}
\todo{What is this thesis about? (Mechanizing semantic proofs)} \noindent
The primary objective of this work is to determine the "semantic gap"
between human-readable proofs and machine-checked logic.
Specifically, this thesis seeks to answer the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Do the proof techniques presented in the 1DL311 curriculum
    map directly to Lean's internal mechanisms, or do they require significant
    structural reformulation?
    \item \textbf{RQ2:} What implicit assumptions or "pedagogical shortcuts"
    present in the literature must be made explicit to satisfy the Lean kernel?
\end{itemize}

\label{intro:motivation}
\section{Motivation}
\todo{Why does it matter? (Informal proofs hide complexity)} \noindent
Informal proofs are designed for human consumption and often prioritize
intuition over exhaustive detail. However, these omissions can hide
underlying complexities or logical leaps. By identifying these points of
divergence, this research provides a roadmap for how educators can
structure pen-and-paper proofs to decrease ambiguity and better prepare students
for formal verification tasks.

\section{Programming Language Semantics: Four Approaches}
\label{background:sec:pls}
\todo{Introduce Programming Language Semantics and the 2 (4 if we got time) diffrent approaches}
There are four major approaches to construct semantics for a programming language, of which we will examine proofs of two of them.
In this section we will do a quick overview of thier diffrences.
First approach is called "Natural Semantics" often refered to as "Big-Step Semantics" and often shortend to "ns" which we will explore in \autoref{background:subsec:ns}.
Secound approach is called "Structural Operational Semantics" which is refered to as "Small-Step Semantics" and shortend to "sos" explored in \autoref{background:subsec:sos}.

The third approach which will not be exaxmined in this thesis is "Axiomatic Semantics".

Lastly we have "Denotational Semantics" which also wont be studied in this thesis.

We will also do a quick overview of the toy language "While" introduced by the litterature "Semantics with Applications An Appetizer" in \autoref{background:subsec:while_lang}.
