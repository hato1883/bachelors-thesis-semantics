\chapter{Related Work}
The mechanization of programming language semantics requires navigating a
trade-off between the high-level intuition of pedagogical materials and the
low-level rigor of a logical kernel. This research is situated among several
key efforts to bridge this "semantic gap."

%
%
\section{Educational Abstractions vs.General Purpose Provers}
To lower the entry barrier for students, tools like
SASyLF~\citep{SASyLF} provide a specialized environment where proofs are constructed in a
"math-like" syntax. SASyLF~\citep{SASyLF}
prioritizes the student's mental model by
abstracting away much of the "syntactic noise" identified in Point 1. In
contrast, this thesis uses Lean 4, a general-purpose theorem prover.

By doing so, we evaluate what is lost when these educational abstractions are
removed, specifically regarding the "pedagogical shortcuts" identified in RQ2.

\section{The Variable Binding Problem and Scoping}
The difficulty of formalizing
variable binding—the "infrastructure" of names and scopes—is the central
theme of the POPLMark Challenge~\citep{aydemir_mechanized_2005}. Traditional solutions often involve complex
techniques, such as Nominal Techniques in Isabelle/HOL~\citep{urban_nominal_Isabelle_2008}, which automate the
handling of $\alpha$-equivalence and variable freshness. However, as noted in
the analysis of the textbook~\citep{nielson_semantics_2007}, the target language assumes a Global
Scope with no variable shadowing or undefined variables. Consequently, this
work intentionally bypasses the binding complexities discussed in the POPLMark~\citep{aydemir_mechanized_2005} 
literature. This allows for a focused evaluation of other "verbatim" translation
issues, such as:The transition from intuitive side conditions to explicit
premises (Point 2).
The requirement for decidable equality on variable strings (Point 3).

\section{Engineering the "Trivial": Scaffolding and Tactics}
Even in a language with
simple scope, the engineering effort required to satisfy a kernel remains high.

\begin{itemize}
    \item QED at Large~\citep{ringer_qed_2019}: This survey of formally verified software highlights that
"proof engineering"—the creation of scaffolding lemmas—is often the most
time-consuming part of formalization. This aligns with my finding in Point 5,
where textbook logic for state updates ($s[x \to v]$) requires multiple "hidden"
lemmas in Lean to prove identity and extensionality.

    \item The C Standard Formalized in Coq~\citep{mechanizedCSpecification}: On a larger scale, the formalization of the
C standard (e.g., CH2O~\citep{mechanizedCSpecification}) demonstrates how informal prose must be turned into a
rigid, constructive logic. This study mirrors that process on a pedagogical
scale, investigating how "trivial" textbook assumptions must be made explicit.
\end{itemize}

\section{Modern Formalization Frameworks}
The recent Modular Lean 4 Framework for Lambda
Calculi~\citep{ramos2025modularLean4framework} provides a look at how Lean 4 can be used to build highly structured,
re-usable metatheory. While that framework focuses on the complexity of products,
sums, and normalization, my work focuses on fidelity to the curriculum. While a
modular framework re-engineers the logic for efficiency in the prover, my "verbatim"
approach tests whether Lean can be used as a direct mirror for existing educational
literature.

\section{Automation and the Poincaré Principle}
Finally, the gap in reasoning
about numbers (Point 4) is bridged in this work using Lean's \texttt{linarith} tactic.
This follows the "Poincaré Principle"~\citep{barendregt_autarkic_2002}, the idea that trivial computational steps
should be handled by the machine rather than the user. By utilizing \texttt{linarith},
we ensure that the focus of the formalization remains on the semantics of the
language rather than the intricacies of natural number induction, which is not
the primary focus of the textbook~\citep{nielson_semantics_2007}.
