\chapter{Discussion: Mechanizing Semantic Proofs — Findings and Insights}

This chapter summarizes the results found in the previous chapter and discusses
them in relation to the research questions. While the previous chapter provided
a detailed discussion of individual proofs and mechanization's, this
chapter takes a step back to evaluate the collective findings.
We repeat the research question here:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1:} Do the proof techniques presented in the textbook~\citep{nielson_semantics_2007}
    map directly to Lean's internal mechanisms, or do they require significant
    structural reformulation?

    \item \textbf{RQ2:} What implicit assumptions or "pedagogical shortcuts"
    present in the literature must be made explicit to satisfy the Lean proof assistant?
\end{itemize}

\section{The Syntax-Logic Interface: From Strings to Structures}
\label{discussion:sec:syntax_logic}

This section primarily addresses RQ2. The key finding is that many apparently
"simple" definitional choices in textbook semantics rely on computational
assumptions that Lean requires us to state and prove.

\subsection*{The Myth of the "Simple String"}
The \emph{Semantics with Applications: An Appetizer} textbook presentations treat variables as abstract identifiers and proceed as
if equality between names is always available. In Lean, this shortcut becomes
an explicit requirement: variable types must provide \texttt{DecidableEq} so
that state updates and conditional checks can compute. This is a direct example
of RQ2: a pedagogical convenience in prose (``we can always tell whether
$x = y$") becomes a formalization burden.

\subsection*{Tree-Based Reality: BNF to Inductive Types}
BNF is a textual grammar listing production rules,
a parse tree (or abstract syntax tree, AST) is the hierarchical
structure produced by those rules.
When mechanizing we work with ASTs encoded as Lean inductive types,
so proofs operate by case analysis and structural induction on constructors.
This is not a substantive change in logic, BNF generates the same set of trees,
but it does change proof organization and style.

\section{Operational Semantics: Configurations and the Notation Gap}
\label{discussion:sec:operational_notation}

This section addresses both RQ1 and RQ2 by contrasting how semantic transitions
map into Lean and the technical hurdles of maintaining readable notation.

\subsection*{Natural Semantics: High-Fidelity Mapping}
Big-step rules exhibit the highest mapping density: the derivation rules used in the
textbook \emph{Semantics with Applications: An Appetizer}~\citep{nielson_semantics_2007} corresponds closely to Lean constructors for the evaluation relation.
In this sense, RQ1 is answered positively at the logical level. However, side
conditions introduce an explicitness burden (RQ2). Conditions such as
$\mathcal{B}[\![b]\!] s = \mathbf{tt}$, often treated as marginal annotations
in textbook derivations, become mandatory premises. Rules that are informally 
`inapplicable'' (e.g., the \textit{false} case of an \textit{if} statement)
still appear as formal cases and must be closed by contradiction.

\subsection*{Small-Step (SOS): The Configuration Hurdle}
Small-step semantics reveal a stronger reformulation burden.
The textbook notation fluidly shifts between intermediate transitions
($\langle S, s \rangle \Rightarrow \langle S', s' \rangle$) and terminal states
($\langle S, s \rangle \Rightarrow s'$). Lean requires this distinction to be
explicit via a \texttt{Config} type separating active commands from terminal states. 

Mechanizing this requires managing the permutations of transitions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $(Stmt \times State) \Rightarrow (Stmt \times State)$
    \item $(Stmt \times State) \Rightarrow State$
    \item $(Stmt \times State)  \Rightarrow Config$
    \item $Config \Rightarrow (Stmt \times State)$
    \item $Config \Rightarrow State$
    \item $Config  \Rightarrow Config$
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The Weight of Ambiguity: Priority and Overloading}
On paper, the same arrow symbol $\Rightarrow$ is used for all the transitions above;
readers use visual context to disambiguate the target. For the Lean elaborator,
this creates a significant \textbf{overloading problem}. Without explicit type
annotations or a single unified sum-type for configurations, the compiler cannot
determine which inductive relation to invoke.

We addressed this by picking a specific subset of notations for the most frequent transitions.
For the remaining permutations, we required the user to encode the transitions manually
using the underlying relations \texttt{small\_step\_k} or \texttt{small\_step\_star}
and explicit configuration constructors such as \texttt{Config.step (S, s)} or
\texttt{Config.final s}. This highlights a key RQ2 finding: pedagogical notation
relies on a ``human type-checker'' that can resolve ambiguity through intuition, a 
luxury that Lean does not share. By forcing explicit constructor calls in
complex cases, the mechanization ensures that the exact nature of the configuration
transition is never in doubt.

\section{The Equivalence Theorem: Managing Complexity}
\label{discussion:sec:equivalence_complexity}

The proof of equivalence between the Natural Semantics and the Structural Operational Semantics
makes the explicitness gap most visible because it
combines inductive structure, transitivity, and arithmetic on derivation length.

\subsection*{From NS to SOS: Transitivity and Intermediate States}
In the $NS \to SOS$ direction, textbook ellipses (``$\dots$") hide intermediate
configurations. Lean does not permit this compression: every intermediate state
must be provided, and every composition step justified through explicit
transitivity lemmas (e.g., \texttt{small\_step\_star\_trans}). This is a concrete
RQ2 result: omitted intermediate states are a pedagogical shortcut that
mechanization removes.

\subsection*{From SOS to NS: Why Strong Induction Is Necessary}
In the $SOS \to NS$ direction, induction ``on derivation length'' requires an explicit
arithmetic scaffold. In Lean this appears as \texttt{Nat.strongRecOn} plus
arithmetic side goals discharged by tactics such as \texttt{linarith}.
The textbook can just discharge arithmetic components as trivial, leaning on the assumption
of that addition is trivial to proof, something we must rely on well constructed tactics to prove.

\section{Synthesis and Answers to the Research Questions}

\subsection*{Answer to RQ1: Mapping vs. Reformulation}
The logical hierarchy maps well, but the procedural flow requires reformulation.
The biggest shift is from \textit{narrative} proofs to \textit{tactic-driven} proofs.
Lean forces the closure of "impossible" branches that textbooks ignore, and demands
explicit handling of notation priority which is otherwise handled by human intuition.

\subsection*{Answer to RQ2: The Main Pedagogical Shortcuts}
The mechanization reveals four recurring shortcuts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Identifier Identity}: Equality of names requires \texttt{DecidableEq}.

    \item \textbf{State Consistency}: The "Shadowing Lemma" must be proven, not assumed.

    \item \textbf{Notation Compression}: The use of a single arrow for multiple
    configuration types requires explicit priority/weights for the compiler.

    \item \textbf{Transitivity Ellipses}: Intermediate execution steps (the ``$\dots$'')
    must be fully materialized.
\end{itemize}
