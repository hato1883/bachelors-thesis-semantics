\chapter{Discussion: Mechanizing Semantic Proofs — Findings and Insights}

This chapter synthesizes the side-by-side comparison developed in the method
chapter: for each selected result, the mechanized Lean proof is aligned with its
textbook counterpart from 1DL311. The goal is to answer two research questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1}: Do the proof techniques from 1DL311 map directly to Lean's internal mechanisms, or do they require structural reformulation?
    \item \textbf{RQ2}: Which implicit assumptions or pedagogical shortcuts in the literature must be made explicit to satisfy the Lean kernel?
\end{itemize}

The discussion is organized around recurring transformation patterns observed
across syntax, semantics, and equivalence proofs.

\section{The Syntax-Logic Interface: From Strings to Structures}
\label{discussion:sec:syntax_logic}

This section primarily addresses RQ2. The key finding is that many apparently
"simple" definitional choices in textbook semantics rely on computational
assumptions that Lean requires us to state and prove.

\subsection*{The Myth of the "Simple String"}

Textbook presentations treat variables as abstract identifiers and proceed as if
equality between names is always available. In Lean, this shortcut becomes an
explicit requirement: variable types must provide \texttt{DecidableEq} so that
state updates and conditional checks can compute.

This is a direct example of RQ2: a pedagogical convenience in prose
(``we can always tell whether $x = y$") becomes a kernel-level obligation.

\subsection*{Tree-Based Reality: BNF to Inductive Types}

The move from BNF notation to Lean inductive definitions is mostly faithful in
logical content, but not neutral in structure. BNF is read linearly; Lean terms
are constructor trees. As a consequence, proofs naturally become structural:
induction follows the datatype shape rather than the order of textbook prose.

For RQ1, this means that the underlying logic maps well, while the procedural
presentation is reformulated around tree recursion and constructor cases.

\subsection*{State Updates and the Shadowing Lemma}

On paper, an update like $s[x \mapsto v]$ is often treated as an intuitive
operation. In Lean, its behavior under repeated updates must be made explicit.
The shadowing lemma is therefore not optional bookkeeping; it is a consistency
result that certifies that overwriting a variable behaves as intended.

This is another RQ2 finding: state consistency is not implied by notation, but
secured by explicit lemmas.

\section{Operational Semantics and the Explicitness Gap}
\label{discussion:sec:operational_gap}

This section addresses both RQ1 and RQ2 by contrasting how big-step and
small-step semantics map into Lean.

\subsection*{Big-Step (Natural) Semantics: High-Fidelity Mapping}

Big-step rules exhibit the highest mapping density: the derivation tree used in
1DL311 corresponds closely to Lean constructors for the evaluation relation.
In this sense, RQ1 is answered positively at the logical level.

However, side conditions introduce an explicitness burden (RQ2). Conditions
such as \(\mathcal{B}[\![b]\!]s = \mathbf{tt}\), often treated as annotations
in textbook derivations, become mandatory premises that must be discharged.
Rules that are informally ``inapplicable'' still appear as formal cases and must
be closed by contradiction.

\subsection*{Small-Step (SOS) Semantics: The Configuration Hurdle}

Small-step semantics reveal a stronger reformulation burden. The overloaded
arrow notation in prose leaves context to indicate whether execution is finished.
Lean requires this distinction to be explicit via a configuration type separating
active commands from terminal states.

The result is a clear RQ2 insight: textbook notation compresses semantic state.
Mechanization decomposes that compression into explicit constructors and
transition judgments.

\section{Proof Tactics and Structural Reformulation}
\label{discussion:sec:proof_tactics}

This section focuses on RQ1: many textbook proof moves survive in intent but
not in operational form.

\subsection*{The Death of ``Analogously'': Closing Impossible Cases}

In course-level proofs, impossible branches are often omitted by inspection.
Lean's \texttt{cases} and \texttt{induction} tactics force complete branch coverage.
When a branch contradicts known premises, that contradiction must be shown
explicitly (for instance by rewriting a boolean side condition and invoking
\texttt{contradiction}).

This is a structural reformulation: ``obvious omission'' in prose becomes
``formal absurdity proof'' in Lean.

\subsection*{State Unification vs. Narrative Flow}

Textbook proofs narrate state evolution at a high level. Mechanized proofs must
perform substitutions and unification steps that align syntactic forms exactly.
What appears as a trivial variable replacement in prose can correspond to
nontrivial rewriting and dependent matching in the kernel.

\subsection*{Lemmas as Bridges: While Unrolling}

Loop unrolling illustrates a compositional advantage of mechanization.
The biconditional argument is split into directed lemmas (e.g.,
\texttt{while\_expand} and \texttt{if\_compact}) and then reassembled.
Compared with narrative proofs, this produces reusable proof components and
clear interfaces between argument steps.

\section{The Equivalence Theorem: Managing Complexity}
\label{discussion:sec:equivalence_complexity}

The proof of $NS \equiv SOS$ makes the explicitness gap most visible because it
combines inductive structure, transitivity, and arithmetic on derivation length.

\subsection*{From NS to SOS: Transitivity and Intermediate States}

In the $NS \to SOS$ direction, textbook ellipses (``$\cdots$") hide intermediate
configurations. Lean does not permit this compression: every intermediate state
must be provided and every composition step justified through explicit transitivity
lemmas (e.g., \texttt{small\_step\_star\_trans}).

This gives a concrete RQ2 result: omitted intermediate states are a pedagogical
shortcut that mechanization removes.

\subsection*{From SOS to NS: Why Strong Induction Is Necessary}

In the $SOS \to NS$ direction, induction ``on derivation length'' requires an
explicit arithmetic scaffold. In Lean this appears as \texttt{Nat.strongRecOn}
plus arithmetic side goals discharged by tools such as \texttt{linarith}.
Case splits for composition and loop rules must also be made explicit and linked
to decomposition lemmas.

The conclusion for RQ1 is clear: the proof idea maps, but its execution requires
significant structural reformulation.

\section{Synthesis and Answers to the Research Questions}
\label{discussion:sec:synthesis}

\subsection*{Answer to RQ1: Mapping vs. Reformulation}

The logical hierarchy of 1DL311 proofs maps well to Lean: judgments, rules,
and induction principles remain recognizable. However, procedural proof flow
requires structural reformulation. Informal prose moves are replaced by tactic-
level operations: explicit case splits, unification steps, witness construction,
and compositional lemma application.

\subsection*{Answer to RQ2: The Main Pedagogical Shortcuts}

The mechanization reveals three recurring shortcuts that must be made explicit:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Identifier Identity}: Equality of variable names requires explicit
    decidable equality assumptions.
    \item \textbf{State Consistency}: Updates and substitutions require dedicated
    lemmas (e.g., shadowing), not only notation.
    \item \textbf{Arithmetic of Sequences}: Length-based induction in small-step
    proofs needs explicit natural-number reasoning.
\end{itemize}

Across all sections, one unifying theme emerges: mechanization is not a mere
translation of symbols. It is a disciplined expansion of informal conventions
into explicit, checkable proof obligations.

\section{Lessons for Future Mechanization}
\label{discussion:sec:lessons}

Based on the mechanization experience, several lessons emerge for future work.

\subsection*{Planning and Structuring Proofs}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Identify Proof Patterns Early}:
    Recognize whether the proof has a recurring layout such as
    structural (shape of derivations via \texttt{induction})
    or arithmetic (length of sequences via \texttt{Nat.strongRecOn}).
    
    \item \textbf{Lemmatize Aggressively}:
    Break large proofs into smaller, reusable lemmas. What appears as
    informal appeal in textbooks should become explicit lemma calls.
    
    \item \textbf{Generalization and Witnesses}:
    When setting up induction, explicitly generalize variables that should not be
    fixed. Plan how to unpack and specialize existential witnesses via pattern matching.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Handling Side Conditions and Automation}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Enumerate Side Conditions}:
    Cases ruled out by side conditions must be shown to lead to contradictions
    via explicit \texttt{rw} and \texttt{contradiction} tactics.
    
    \item \textbf{Rely on Automation Where Possible}:
    Use \texttt{simp} and \texttt{linarith} liberally for routine bookkeeping,
    but separate non-arithmetic reasoning from arithmetic reasoning for clarity.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Relations Over Partial Functions}

Work with evaluation relations as the primary object rather than partial functions.
While partial behavior can be encoded in several ways, relations are usually
more tractable for semantic equivalence arguments and align directly with the
proof obligations used in this thesis.
